Ecosystem

We set out in February to bring technology, the community and City Hall together to make life better for everyone in Chattanooga. Looking around, we hypothesized that our most impactful approach would be to bolster the burgeoning open data ecosystem in the Gig City. Naturally occurring ecosystems sustain themselves because of the pressure applied to different parts of the system. By focusing on a few different initiatives we aimed to set the open data ecosystem in Chattanooga on a path to future success.

Local Wiki

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet consectetur.

Open data can come from city hall or non-profits, or it can come from the community. When it comes from members of the community, it can be more helpful to think about it as “open knowledge”, information for the community written by the community. During CodeAcross 2014 we launched ChaWiki, the LocalWiki just for Chattanooga. In just the few months that followed, over 330 pages were created about topics ranging from histories of various neighborhoods to lists of places with the best happy hours to an extensive catalog of internet speeds all over Chattanooga. The transportation department and regional planning agency even experimented with using the wiki as a way to gather feedback on planned bike routes. The LocalWiki continues to be utilized by community groups across the city as a way to document local history, share opinions or just learn new things about their community.

BestNest

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet consectetur.

BestNest is an application, built on top of open data released by the city, that helps renters in Chattanooga understand more about the homes they might live in. It does this by showing anybody in Chattanooga how many code violations.

We used the application as a way to start conversations about open data with departments inside of City Hall and within the county government. In the beginning, many people were less than enthused about opening up data but as the year progressed and we had more to show for it, the process got easier and easier.

We also wanted to demonstrate the need to bring community members into the process early, so we developed the app with continuous feedback from Emily O’Donnell, a housing rights lawyer with Legal Aid of East Tennessee. As we got closer and closer to a prototype that we wanted to show, we need to engage more community partners to help us reach out to our target users.

The local Catholic Charities chapter puts on a program for low-income renters in cooperation with the Chattanooga Housing Authority called S.M.A.R.T. It is a multi-day training program where the renters learn about budgeting, landlord relations, and things to be aware of as a renter in Chattanooga. We approached Catholic Charities about launching BestNest at the S.M.A.R.T. training and getting actionable feedback from the participants. They were thrilled that we would be building something that could help their community and provided us with a platform to launch BestNest. On October 30, 2014 we presented BestNest to about 25 renters in Chattanooga.

BestNest will be handed off to Open Chattanooga for them to maintain. They are excited about owning the future of BestNest because it gives them a way to engage community members that aren’t very involved in the Brigade to date, such as Catholic Charities and other housing rights advocacy organizations. It also provides them with a well designed application to onboard developers who are interested in helping out the Brigade but are a bit unsure of how to plug in.

BestNest could not be in more capable hands. As the stewards of the open data catalog, the Brigade is in the position to ensure that the data presented in the application is always as up to date as possible.

Check out BestNest for Chattanooga BestNestApp.com.

Transit Data

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet consectetur.

Open data can sometimes seem abstract, and the benefits for regular residents aren’t always apparent. We wanted to make sure to release a high-value dataset that would form the basis for apps that truly benefit the people of Chattanooga.

We worked together with the transit agency in Chattanooga to release their bus schedules in an open data format known as GTFS. This is the same data format that is used by Google Maps for public transportation routing and a whole ecosystem of third party transit apps have grown up around it.

Once we were able to publish the data, some app developers in Montreal noticed pretty quickly and within a few days they had integrated Chattanooga into their popular TransitApp. As we bragged about during the Code for America summit, this app integration took three days, no budget and zero lines of code written by the City and resulted in the first mobile, multi-modal trip planning tool in Chattanooga’s history.

It was rewarding to see open data finally make sense to many people inside of City Hall. If you publish valuable, clean data and invite collaboration then you can have an outsized, and on going, impact. In fact, during the transit agency board meeting where Jenny Park presented our work around Transit App, a second app announced that it had integrated Chattanooga as well. This time, though, it was pleasantly surprising news to us as well.

You can download Transit App for iphone or android.
